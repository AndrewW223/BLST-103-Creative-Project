The Black Freedom Struggle Post-1965

Beyond the Ballot

The passage of the Voting Rights Act of 1965 represented the culmination of the southern struggle to eliminate Jim Crow and secure legal enfranchisement. This law was a monumental triumph, but it quickly became clear that simply having the right to vote would not solve systemic poverty, structural racism, and state violence.

Following 1965, the Black Freedom movement pivoted dramatically. The geographic center shifted from the rural South to the urban North and West. Ideologically, the focus shifted away from seeking legal integration into mainstream white society, moving instead toward cultural autonomy, intersectional liberation, institutional power, and the dismantling of systemic anti-Black violence.
